🧬 In Silico Assessment of Aspirin Binding Dynamics within SARS-CoV-2 Main Protease (Mpro)

This project presents a comprehensive computational investigation into the binding interaction between SARS-CoV-2 main protease (Mpro) and Aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid) using molecular docking approaches. The Mpro enzyme is a critical component in the viral life cycle, responsible for processing polyproteins into functional units necessary for viral replication, making it a well-established target for antiviral drug discovery. In this study, the crystal structure of Mpro (PDB ID: 9O6F), specifically the S113A mutant complexed with an inhibitor, was retrieved from the Protein Data Bank and prepared by removing co-crystallized ligands and solvent molecules to expose the catalytic binding site. The prepared protein structure served as the target for docking simulations aimed at evaluating the binding affinity and structural compatibility of Aspirin within the active site.

The computational workflow integrates widely used structural biology tools, including the Protein Data Bank for structure acquisition, AutoDock Vina as the docking engine, and UCSF ChimeraX for visualization and structural analysis. During the docking phase, a grid box was defined around the catalytic pocket to restrict the conformational search space, and AutoDock Vina was employed to generate multiple ligand binding poses. Each pose was scored based on predicted binding free energy (ΔG, kcal/mol), where more negative values indicate stronger thermodynamic favorability. The docking results converged on a best binding affinity of -4.916 kcal/mol, suggesting a modest but stable interaction between Aspirin and the protease. The consistency of energy values across multiple poses indicates reasonable convergence of the docking simulation.

Subsequent structural analysis using UCSF ChimeraX revealed that Aspirin occupies a well-defined cavity within the Mpro binding pocket, aligning with chemically compatible regions of the protein. The ligand orientation and positioning suggest that stabilization of the complex is achieved through spatial complementarity and local interactions within the binding site. Visualization of internal cavities and energy distribution plots further confirmed that the ligand consistently localizes within the same pocket, reinforcing the reliability of the docking predictions. The repository supporting this project includes essential files such as the input protein structure (9O6F.pdb), docking result files containing affinity scores, cavity visualization sessions, and graphical plots of energy distributions, all of which can be explored using ChimeraX.

Although the observed binding affinity indicates only a weak-to-moderate interaction, the findings provide a valuable baseline for exploring drug repurposing strategies involving common anti-inflammatory compounds. It is important to note that molecular docking alone does not account for protein flexibility, solvent effects, or dynamic conformational changes, and therefore the results should be interpreted as preliminary. Future work could extend this study by incorporating molecular dynamics simulations, binding free energy calculations such as MM-PBSA or MM-GBSA, and comparative analysis with known high-affinity Mpro inhibitors. Overall, this project demonstrates a reproducible and structured computational pipeline for assessing protein–ligand interactions and highlights the potential of leveraging existing drugs as starting points for antiviral research.
